1. Indus Village overview

Image source: Minecraft Wiki (© Mojang Studios), via Gamepedia
First, a brief overview of our modelling programme and the advancements made so far.

End-goals

Explore human adaptation to the variability
in landscapes of NW India during the Mature Harappan (urban phase of the Indus Civilisation)

Expose the sustainability of food production regimes,
mainly in terms of cropping strategies,
in front of climate change

The Two Rains modelling programme was set to explore human adaptation to the variability in landscapes of NW India during the Mature Harappan. Specifically, we focused on the surroundings of the sites in Haryana studied by Two Rains and former projects. | Our aim is to expose the sustainability of food production regimes, especially in terms of the diversity of cropping strategies, and if and how this diversity was favourable or adverse to urbanisation.
First, we defined what we believe are essential elements of rural, food-producing settlements. Our approach was to re-imagine an integral, yet blurry picture of what village life in the Indus would look like, based on known archaeological evidence, but also on minimum assumptions informed by parallels in space and time. | In a broad sense, these elements include: | Climate: more specifically the weather conditions related to solar radiation, temperature and precipitation. | Land: which includes soil, water and cover. | Food production: mainly crops, which translate the condition of land and climate into foodstuff. | A human population: organised in households and groups and inhabiting a settlement. | The demography rulling the dynamics of this population, | The nutritional budget that regulates its health, | The diet that feeds into it, | and the food production economy that describes how production, distribution and storage of foodstuffs happens. | A exchange system that connects agents to other agents, and to agents in settlements outside the local economy. | And finally, bonding all together, the decision-making process that households would go through to chose and revise their food production strategies, particularly in terms of the triplet use-conditions-investment. | Everything strictly within a small scale, with one-hectare land units covering a total area of twenty-five kilometres squared, and with processes being iterated up to a daily basis.

Modular design
  • Implementation in NetLogo, documentation in pseudocode diagrams and R markdown
  • parameter exploration (sensitivity analysis)
  • scenarios (parameters, programmed trends
    and changes, alternative algorithms)
Deriving from this general picture, we represented all these aspects as submodels or parts of submodels, highlighted here in blue. These submodels are intentionally designed as modules, meaning they can function independently as models, if the required input is given. Besides the advantage in terms of code stability and extensibility, our hope is that each of these submodels can be more easily re-used by others in the future. | The full version of the Indus Village model is still under development. Here I will concentrate on those parts that are fully consolidated and explored. We are thus leaving aside the submodels dealing strictly with human-related mechanisms. | The model is implemented in NetLogo in a modular and progressive manner, while each part is also thoroughly documented with pseudo-code diagrams and R markdown demonstrations. | To properly understand the model at each stage of complexity, we are performing extensive sensitivity analyses. | To inform and test our hypotheses, we perform experiments designed to represent scenarios, particularly by varying parameters, programming trends and changes, and using alternative designs.

2. Food economy in the Indus Civilisation

Image source: Lhendup G Bhutia in openthemagazine.com (© Mojang Studios)
Next, we will briefly present the what do we know about the Indus Civilisation food economy, and what are the challenges to be faced in terms of design choices and data input. We will then do an overview of the concepts behind the submodels we use to represent the food economy cycle and how they are combined into a higher integrated model, stepping towards the full integration of the Indus Village model.

Petrie & Bates 2017 | Bates 2019 | Bates 2020 | Bates, Petrie & Singh 2018


Archaeobotany

  • barley/wheat (winter)
  • millet/rice (summer)
  • Pulses and other crops
  • Non-crops (e.g. fruits)
  • Plants for other uses

Agriculture was likely the main pilar of the Indus food economy. The crops cultivated were ancient varieties of wheat and barley (grown during winter or rabi season), various millets and rice (grown in pre and post monsoon or zaid and kharif seasons), among many others. One key aspect raised in previous research is how farmers managed seasonality and climate variation, particularly if and how multi-cropping was performed.


Zooarchaeology

  • Animal husbandry:
    zebu, water buffalo, sheep, goat, pig(?), chicken(?)
  • Fishing: freshwater fish and molluscs
  • Hunting: wild ruminants, hare, birds

Joglekar, Sharada & Abhayan 2013 | Chase, Meiggs & Ajithprasad 2020
Abhayan et al. 2020 | Lightfoot et al. 2020 | LJ Miller 2004 | RP Wright 2010
Role of herding?
Animal husbandry is also thought to have been central in the Indus food economy. together with other activities such as hunting and fishing, which are still widely present today. Particularly, several authors have raised the hypothesis of mid-to-long distance herding as having a role not only in food economy, but in shaping trade and cultural diversity the region.


Residue Analysis

  • C3 vs C4 plants signature
  • Meat vs secondary products
  • Ruminant vs non-ruminant fat

Suryanarayan et al. 2021
Stewing or roasting?
Are contemporary culinary practices a good reference?
Other bioarchaeological analyses have contributed to our understanding of Indus foodways, but also raised additional challenges to be tackled in the future, such as, in lipid analyses, the confounding effect of C3/C4 plants as direct ingredients or as fodder of animals under different feeding regimes. A polemic still surrounds the validity of holding certain contemporary culinary practices as references to understanding those in the ancient Indus.

Food exchange


Household autonomy, local reciprocity and/or market-like economy?
RP Wright 2010
Food redistribution is an interesting, yet widely unexplored aspect. There are, of course, abundant evidence for exchange of a variety of materials, including organic materials, reaching even relatively far partners, such as the Sumerian cities. Would food exchange be relevant to the subsistence of villages though?





3. Household Food Economy model

Image source: Danica Torreja in gamersdecide.com (© Mojang Studios)
We are currently working on the integration of the next milestone model, the Household Food Economy model, which aims at a minimum yet sufficient representation of the dynamics in food economy at the household level.
The model targets the food economy cycle defined as a sequence of processes happening on a household scale, spanning from the obtention of foodstuffs to the disposition of labour and dietary preferences towards food production.
The Household Food Economy model integrates five submodels: the Food Strategy, Food Storage, Food Exchange, Nutrition, and Household Demography models

Food Storage model


Households obtain a series of foodstuff amounts in different moments of the annual calendar through different processes modelled in other parts of the Indus Village, particularly the Land Crop model. These amounts are accounted for as stocks of foodstuff that are stored, exchanged, and consumed. Food consumption is calculated daily for the entire household based on its members, and assuming that households pursue the satisfaction of a diet. Diet is here a composition of foodstuff amounts and it is modelled as a cultural trait specific to households.

Food Storage model



Link to code (repository) | documentation under development

Food Exchange model


Households can also engage on food exchange, as "sellers", if having surplus, or "buyers", if having shortage. After each stock-sized transaction is made, they may complement their diet, if lacking in the first place. There are still open questions regarding the terms of exchange transactions. As a preliminary approach, we are distinguishing local exchange as being based on open reciprocity or based on a voluntary redistribution institution. In the long term, we are interested in how other, possibly overlapping, exchange systems such as market-like economies affected the local food economy.

Nutrition model

The food effectively consumed is translated into nutrient amounts, and distributed among household members. Nutrient amounts are compared to the members' specific sex-age requirements, producing a household-level nutrition score.

Household Demography model


Next, the nutrition score is used to condition the dynamics of a individual-based demographic simulation, the Household Demography model. The Household Demography model relies on a simplified representation of individual household members, however keeping track of individual properties, particularly sex and age. This approach also allows us to hold information of life stories that are key to model realistically social relationships within and among households.

Fertility & Nuptiality: Peristeva & Kostaki 2007; 2015
Mortality: Coale, Demeny, & Vaughan 1983 | Jones 2007, demogR package
The Household Demography model is in fact composed by three separate equation-based submodels, the Fertility, Nuptiality and Mortality models, which output the probability of an individual, respectively, to give birth (female), form a stable couple, or die, depending on their sex and age. The household's nutrition score modulates the mortality model, increasing or decreasing the reference probability of dying per each sex-age category.

Household Demography model

Effect of nutrition over mortality
The nutrition score is used to condition the outcome of the mortality submodel following an algorithmic approach (which is a preliminary solution) where a negative score will increase linearly the probability of dying while a positive score will decrease it with diminishing intensity.

Calculation of labour

Each household holds an ever-changing population, from which an age-dependent amount of labour is made available, part of which is invested back into food production activities.

Food Strategy model


To close the food economy cycle, the Food Strategy model determines how households apply a learning algorithm to steer their food production strategy, specifically regarding which activities to invest labour in, considering diet satisfaction as the fundamental target.

Food Strategy model


Design principles:
  • reinforced learning
  • stimulus for change as degree of diet shortages
  • food strategy → triplet use/activity, conditions, investment
  • challenge: multidimensional state space, tryouts with high stakes
